In part one of this series, I asked Claude to write a blog post about Los Angeles as if it were me. After giving it a minimal blurb to give it the jumpstart it needed, it produced an elaborate piece about LA that seemed to be a cross between a travel guide and a Wikipedia page. Nevertheless, it was a well-informed, first-person reflection on “my” love and appreciation for Los Angeles. This inspired me to have a full conversation with my recently converted Angeleo LLM to understand what it would recommend for a first-time traveler to LA.

The conversation

Instead of asking for a first time itinerary for myself, I decided to take on a new persona!

Prompt

I would now like to have a conversation with you as the experienced native Angeleno (as you were in the blog) and me as a first-time traveler to LA

Response

I’d be happy to have that conversation! I’ll take on the role of the experienced native Angeleno from the blog, and you can be the first-time traveler to LA. Let’s get started! Hey there! I heard you’re visiting Los Angeles for the first time - welcome to the City of Angels! As someone who was born and raised here, I’m always excited to help newcomers navigate our sprawling metropolis. What brings you to LA, and how long will you be staying? More importantly, what are you most looking forward to experiencing while you’re here? I’d love to give you some local insights beyond what the travel guides might tell you.

Prompt

hi! I am from arkansas but have always dreamed of moving to california. my dream is to be a star actress and i know the place to make it big is hollywood. i am looking to visit for about 5 days, is that enough?

Response

Great to meet you! Coming from Arkansas to LA is quite a change of scenery. I love your enthusiasm about pursuing acting!

Five days is a decent start to get a taste of LA, but honestly, this city is massive and sprawling - you’ll barely scratch the surface. That said, we can make those five days count to give you both a feel for the acting scene and the general lifestyle here.

For the Hollywood/acting side of things, you might want to check out: - A studio tour (Warner Bros. or Paramount) to see where the magic happens - Walk of Fame and TCL Chinese Theatre (touristy, but a rite of passage) - The Actor’s Key or another workshop space where actors take classes - Catch an improv show at Groundlings or UCB (great places to network too)

But here’s what most people don’t tell you about the “making it in Hollywood” dream - it’s a marathon, not a sprint. Most working actors here spent years hustling, taking classes, building connections, and working day jobs before landing consistent work. The city is filled with talented people from all over the world pursuing the same dream.

While you’re here, I’d also recommend exploring beyond just Hollywood to get a real feel for LA living. Maybe grab breakfast at Sqirl in Silver Lake, hit Venice Beach for the afternoon, and try some Korean BBQ in Koreatown for dinner.

What part of the acting world interests you most? Film, TV, commercials, theater? And are there any particular neighborhoods you’re curious about?

Prompt

Response

I totally get that! Wanting to see the real LA beyond the superficial Hollywood glitz is actually the perfect approach. Let me help you plan a mix of experiences that will show you different sides of the city. For an authentic LA experience, I’d recommend:

Spend a morning like a local - grab coffee at a neighborhood spot like Intelligentsia in Silver Lake or Dinosaur Coffee in Los Feliz, then walk around the reservoir or Griffith Park. This is what many Angelenos do on weekends. Visit the Arts District downtown - it’s where a lot of creatives live and work. Hauser & Wirth gallery, Angel City Brewery, and plenty of murals and indie shops give you a feel for LA’s art scene. Eat where locals eat - skip the touristy places and try a taco truck in Boyle Heights, grab dim sum in San Gabriel Valley, or enjoy Ethiopian food on Fairfax. Our food scene reflects our diversity. Take a drive up the Pacific Coast Highway - rent a car (you’ll need one in LA anyway) and see how the beaches change from Venice (quirky, artistic) to Malibu (serene, upscale). Check out an acting class or drop-in workshop while you’re here - not just as a tourist, but as someone considering the craft. The Actors’ Gang or Anthony Meindl’s workshop might give you insight into what training here is really like.

The reality of LA is that it’s a city of neighborhoods, each with its own character. The “real LA” means different things to different people. Some find their community in the theater scene of North Hollywood, others in the beach cities, and others in the cultural enclaves of places like Little Ethiopia or Historic Filipinotown. What makes you bored in Arkansas? That might help me suggest places in LA that would give you what you’re looking for!

Prompt

i just feel like theres nothing to do. all we do “for fun” is go on drives to nowhere, basically going in circles t pass time. I want to experience real life.

Response

That feeling of going in circles to pass time - I definitely understand the need for something more vibrant and engaging. LA will be a complete 180 from that experience. Here, the challenge is usually deciding what to do among too many options rather than trying to find something to do at all. On any given night, you could:

Catch live music at venues ranging from intimate spots like The Echo to legendary places like The Troubadour See experimental theater in warehouses downtown Join a midnight movie screening at The New Beverly (Tarantino’s theater) Hit up food trucks gathering at a night market Find yourself at a gallery opening where you might bump into all sorts of creative people

What makes LA feel like “real life” to me is that it’s constantly in motion. People are always creating, collaborating, and reinventing themselves. The city has this energy that pushes you forward. But I should also be honest - LA demands a lot from you. It’s expensive, competitive, and can be isolating at first if you don’t find your community. The acting world especially requires thick skin and persistence. Many people come with dreams similar to yours, and the reality is that building a career takes years of consistent work, networking, and handling rejection. During your visit, I’d recommend talking to some working actors if you can - not just the success stories, but the ones who are making a living doing commercials, small TV roles, and theater. They’ll give you the most realistic picture of what the day-to-day hustle is like. What would your ideal day in LA look like? Are you drawn more to the creative energy, the outdoor lifestyle, or the diversity of experiences?

Visit actor-friendly coffee shops like Aroma Café in Studio City or Bourgeois Pig in Franklin Village - you’ll often find people working on scripts or having industry meetings. Check out The Actor’s Key or Actor’s Connection for workshops where working casting directors and agents give feedback.

The reality of LA is that everyone builds their network gradually. Even locals don’t have special access - it’s all about consistently showing up in spaces where creative people gather. The Palisades has this peaceful, secluded feeling despite being so close to the city. The views are spectacular - you’ve got the ocean on one side and mountains on the other.
